\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented a controlled study of prompt sensitivity across six tasks,
eight models (1B--frontier), and 17,378 LLM evaluations.
Through a combination of additive prompt levels, randomised layer-ordering
ablation, and irrelevant-padding controls, we demonstrate that response
quality is governed by \textbf{information sufficiency}: quality saturates
once task-critical content has been communicated, irrespective of raw token
count or layer ordering.

Tasks differ in a \textbf{prompt-sensitivity gradient}: classification gain
is concentrated in a single mechanism (task label), product extraction gain
is distributed across multiple mechanisms, and QA/math are insensitive
(already at ceiling from the bare input).
This gradient, supported by marginal contribution analysis, ceiling
stratification, and dual-judge validation, replaces the binary
structured--open framing and provides concrete guidance for prompt authoring:
identify which prompt mechanisms matter for your task type, include those,
and stop.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Extending the study to additional structured tasks (code generation with type
annotations, data-to-text generation) would test whether the gradient
generalises.
Mechanistic interpretability analyses could directly test the
schema-compliance hypothesis.
Studying saturation in multi-turn and agentic settings---where context
accumulates across turns and tool-call format specifications may exhibit
concentrated sensitivity---would address the increasingly common deployment
pattern of long-horizon LLM agents.
