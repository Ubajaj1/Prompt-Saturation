\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Quality-versus-Token Curves Reveal Task-Dependent Saturation}
\label{sec:result-curves}

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows quality-versus-token curves for all six tasks
and seven models.
Classification curves rise steeply at low token counts and flatten well before
level 7, with four of seven models producing F-test-significant saturating
curves ($p < 0.05$; Table~\ref{tab:ftest}).
Saturation points for these four models range from 42 to 64 tokens,
well below a typical worked-example prompt (level 7: $\approx$300--400 tokens).

Product extraction curves are similarly sigmoidal: three of seven models are
significant, with saturation points ranging from 92 (llama-3.3-70b) to 536
(claude-haiku) tokens.
The wider range reflects greater schema complexity: a four-field JSON
extraction requires more elaboration cues than binary sentiment labelling.
For all significant product extraction pairs, R$^2 > 0.70$, confirming
strong curve fit.

QA and math reasoning yield zero significant pairs out of seven models each.
Fitted curves have low R$^2$ (QA: 0.06--0.69; math: 0.12--0.34) and fail
to beat the flat null model at $\alpha = 0.05$ in every case.
This is a \emph{meaningful negative result}: the same experimental setup
detects strong saturation in classification, ruling out methodological
artefact.

Summarisation (1/7 significant, claude-haiku only) and instruction following
(1/7 significant, llama-3.1-8b only) occupy an intermediate position.

The three models that are most prompt-sensitive in the main study---qwen3-32b,
llama-3.1-8b, and llama-3.3-70b---show robust saturation across orderings:
13/15 fits are significant, with saturation points clustering at 65--140
tokens regardless of which layers come first.
The more prompt-insensitive models (gemini-flash, claude-haiku) show flatter
curves with fewer significant fits (1/10), consistent with these models
reaching high quality from minimal prompting.

The ablation saturation points (65--140 tokens) are substantially lower than
the original fixed-ordering estimates for the same models (92--378 tokens).
This is expected: in the fixed ordering, the most valuable layer for product
extraction---the worked example (contributing 40\% of quality gain; see
Section~\ref{sec:result-marginal})---always comes last, so quality stays low
until high token counts.
In random orderings, this high-value layer can appear at any position,
causing quality to rise sooner and saturation to occur at fewer tokens.
The shift in saturation point directly demonstrates that it is
\emph{information content and ordering}, not raw token count, that determines
where the curve bends.

Classification shows only 2/25 significant fits in the ablation---but this
is expected given classification's prompt-sensitivity profile.
Classification's quality gain is concentrated in a single layer
(Section~\ref{sec:result-marginal}): once the task label appears at any
position, quality jumps and the curve is flat thereafter.
Curve fitting is the wrong tool for detecting a step function; the 2/25
result reflects a method-shape mismatch, not an absence of prompt sensitivity.

\subsection{Padding Control Confirms the Token-Count Null}
\label{sec:result-padding}

The irrelevant-padding experiment provides a direct test: do tokens
themselves buy quality?
Across 1,470 runs, quality with irrelevant padding remained flat or declined
(mean L1$\to$L7 $\Delta = -0.074$ for classification, $-0.013$ for product
extraction).
Only 1 of 26 conditions showed a significant positive trend (Spearman
$p < 0.05$)---a near-ceiling case (claude-haiku classification,
$\Delta = +0.03$, from 0.97 to 1.00) consistent with chance at 26 tests.
Three of 26 significant trends were negative: padding slightly \emph{hurt}
quality.

In contrast, real prompt content over the same token range produced clear
gains: $+0.10$ for classification and $+0.16$ for product extraction.
Table~\ref{tab:padding} shows the per-model comparison.
Real content exceeds padding for 9 of 10 model--task pairs, with the
largest gaps in llama-3.1-8b classification ($+0.190$ vs.\ $-0.161$) and
qwen3-32b product extraction ($+0.220$ vs.\ $-0.038$).

\subsection{Marginal Contributions Reveal the Prompt-Sensitivity Gradient}
\label{sec:result-marginal}

Marginal contribution analysis decomposes quality gain into per-mechanism
contributions, revealing how different tasks distribute their sensitivity
across prompt layers.
Figure~\ref{fig:marginal} shows the per-layer quality deltas averaged
across all seven models.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig2_marginal_contributions.png}
  \caption{Per-layer marginal quality contributions ($\Delta_{k \to k+1}$)
    averaged across seven models.
    Classification gain is concentrated in L1$\to$L2 (task label);
    product extraction gain is distributed across L1$\to$L2 and L6$\to$L7
    (worked example); math reasoning shows a sharp L3 collapse from
    suppressing chain-of-thought.}
  \label{fig:marginal}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Classification (concentrated).}
L1$\to$L2 (adding the task label) contributes $+0.073$ quality, accounting
for 49\% of total positive gain.
Subsequent layers add diminishing returns: L2$\to$L3 (format spec) $+0.017$,
L3$\to$L4 (definitions) $+0.030$, L4$\to$L5 (persona) $-0.020$ (slightly
harmful).
A single well-chosen instruction captures most of the quality benefit.
Output length correspondingly drops from 85.1 tokens (L1, verbose
explanation) to 2.1 tokens (L3+, just the label) as format specification
takes effect---the prompt teaches the model the output schema, and once
learned, further elaboration adds nothing.

\paragraph{Product extraction (distributed).}
Gains accumulate across multiple layers: L1$\to$L2 (field names) $+0.054$
(32\% of gain), L6$\to$L7 (worked example) $+0.067$ (40\% of gain).
The two schema-defining layers together account for 72\% of total
improvement.
The worked example is critical because product extraction requires
demonstrating a complex output format (4-field JSON) that cannot be fully
communicated through format names alone.

\paragraph{Math reasoning (non-monotonic).}
L3 (``Give only the final numerical answer'') causes a dramatic quality
collapse: L2$= 0.967 \to$ L3$= 0.601$ ($\Delta = -0.366$), across all
seven models and both judges.
L4 restores step-by-step reasoning and quality recovers immediately
(L4$= 0.964$).
This non-monotonicity demonstrates that prompt content can be actively
harmful---certain instructions suppress chain-of-thought reasoning, which
is critical for math accuracy~\citep{wei2022cot}.
Excluding L3, the remaining trajectory is flat (L1$= 0.951$, L7$= 0.970$;
$\Delta = +0.019$), consistent with ceiling-at-L1.

\paragraph{QA (insensitive).}
Total marginal gain is essentially zero (L1$\to$L7 $\Delta = -0.035$).
Every layer beyond bare input either adds nothing or slightly hurts---models
already score 0.89--0.95 at L1.

\paragraph{Prompt-sensitivity gradient.}
These profiles define a four-level gradient:
\emph{concentrated} (classification---one layer captures most gain),
\emph{distributed} (product extraction---gains across multiple layers),
\emph{diffuse} (instruction following---small gains across many layers,
largest L5$\to$L6 $= +0.010$),
and \emph{insensitive} (QA, math, summarisation---near-zero or negative
total gain).
Curve fitting detects the distributed pattern (product extraction: 3/7
original significant, 13/15 in ablation) but not the concentrated pattern
(classification: 4/7 original, 2/25 in ablation).
Marginal contribution analysis complements curve fitting by detecting both.

\subsection{Ceiling Stratification Explains Non-Saturation}
\label{sec:result-ceiling}

Why do QA and math show no saturation?
Stratifying by Level-1 quality ($\geq 0.85$ threshold) reveals a ceiling
effect: across models, 16--20 of 20 QA examples already score above 0.85
at L1, leaving too few below-ceiling examples for reliable curve fitting.
The above-ceiling subset shows flat-to-declining quality
(e.g., gemini-flash: L1$=0.94$, L7$=0.91$; mean $\Delta = -0.035$),
consistent with extra layers adding noise to already-correct responses.

Math shows the same ceiling pattern (17--19 of 20 examples above 0.85 at L1)
but with the L3 non-monotonicity superimposed.

This confirms that QA and math ``non-saturation'' is ceiling-at-L1: models
already have sufficient parametric knowledge from the bare question.
Classification and product extraction start below ceiling because the prompt
must communicate format requirements that the model cannot infer from the
input alone.

\paragraph{Below-ceiling stratification.}
Restricting to examples that start below $q = 0.85$ at L1 reveals latent
prompt sensitivity.
Classification below-ceiling examples (32.9\% of model$\times$example
pairs) gain $+0.316$ from L1 to L7; product extraction (92.8\% below
ceiling) gains $+0.167$.
For math reasoning, only 9.4\% of examples start below ceiling, but these
gain $+0.204$---harder math problems \emph{do} respond to prompt
elaboration.
The ``insensitive'' label for QA and math should therefore be qualified:
these tasks are insensitive \textbf{at the ceiling-dominated difficulty
of the current example set}, not necessarily in general.
\emph{Caveat:} below-ceiling examples are selected on low L1 quality, so
part of the positive $\Delta$ may reflect regression to the mean rather
than true prompt sensitivity.

\subsection{Hard Examples Test the Ceiling Artefact}
\label{sec:result-hard}

The below-ceiling stratification (Section~\ref{sec:result-ceiling}) suggests
that ``insensitive'' may reflect easy examples rather than genuine prompt
insensitivity.
We test this directly by re-running three insensitive/diffuse tasks on
harder examples drawn from public datasets: multi-step GSM8K
problems~\citep{cobbe2021gsm8k} (5--8 reasoning steps) for math,
multi-hop HotpotQA questions~\citep{yang2018hotpotqa} for QA,
and multi-constraint instructions (3--5 simultaneous constraints) for
instruction following.
Each task uses 20 hard examples evaluated on three models
(1,260 judge-scored runs total).

\paragraph{QA remains insensitive.}
Hard examples successfully push L1 quality below ceiling
(mean $q_{\text{L1}} = 0.578$ vs.\ $0.918$ on the original set), yet
L1$\to$L7 gain is still near zero ($\Delta = +0.021$).
Prompt elaboration does not help even when models start far from perfect:
QA is genuinely insensitive, not a ceiling artefact.

\paragraph{Instruction following becomes clearly diffuse.}
Hard examples amplify the diffuse signal 6.6$\times$
($\Delta = +0.087$ vs.\ $+0.013$ on originals).
The largest gains come from llama-3.3-70b ($+0.147$) and gemini-flash
($+0.108$), both starting below 0.80 at L1.
Multi-constraint instructions reward the additional prompt layers
(definitions, guidelines) that single-constraint examples cannot
distinguish from noise.

\paragraph{Math is model-dependent.}
Gemini-flash drops to $q_{\text{L1}} = 0.225$ on hard math and gains
$+0.112$ from elaboration; llama-3.3-70b remains at ceiling
($q_{\text{L1}} = 0.988$, $\Delta = -0.003$).
Whether math is prompt-sensitive depends on whether the model can solve
the problem from the bare statement---a function of both problem difficulty
and model capability.

These results refine the gradient taxonomy: QA is robustly insensitive
regardless of difficulty; instruction following is diffuse but requires
sufficiently complex constraints to reveal the pattern; math sensitivity
is determined by the model--difficulty interaction rather than the task
category alone.

\subsection{Model Capability Modulates Saturation}
\label{sec:result-capability}

Within classification, API-tier models (gemini-flash, gpt-4o-mini) saturate
at lower token counts ($\approx$42--50 tokens) than the 70B Llama model
(64 tokens): models that can infer the task from less context reach the
quality plateau sooner.
Within product extraction, the pattern reverses: API-tier models saturate
\emph{later} (claude-haiku at 536 tokens vs.\ llama-3.3-70b at 92 tokens)
but reach \emph{higher} asymptotic quality.
For tasks where the model must \emph{infer} what to do (classification),
more capable models need less prompt.
For tasks where the prompt provides \emph{actionable format specifications}
(extraction), more capable models leverage more of them, continuing to
improve where smaller models plateau early at lower quality.

\paragraph{Sub-8B extension.}
To test whether capability-modulated saturation extends below the 8B floor,
we evaluate Llama-3.2-1B on classification and product extraction
(332 runs).
The 1B model starts significantly lower at L1 (classification: $q=0.467$
vs.\ 0.780 for 8B) and gains 1.58$\times$ more from prompt elaboration
(L1$\to$L7 $\Delta = +0.300$ vs.\ $+0.190$).
For product extraction, the 1B model \emph{completely fails} at L1--L2
($q=0.000$), then jumps to $q=0.776$ at L3 when format specifications are
introduced.
These results extend the capability-modulated trend monotonically from 1B
to 70B+ API-tier models: smaller models are more prompt-sensitive---they
require more elaboration and benefit more from it---but saturate at lower
asymptotic quality.

\subsection{Held-Out Task Refines the Gradient}
\label{sec:result-holdout}

To test whether the prompt-sensitivity gradient generalises beyond the
six main-study tasks, we pre-registered a prediction and evaluated one
held-out task: Named Entity Recognition (NER) on five models.

\paragraph{Prediction.}
NER requires the model to know \emph{which} entity types to extract
(schema) and \emph{how} to format the output (JSON with typed lists)---
structurally analogous to product extraction.
We therefore predicted \textbf{distributed} saturation, with gains
spread across multiple prompt layers.

\paragraph{Results.}
Four of five models produce significant saturating curves
($p < 0.05$; R$^2 > 0.98$ for all four).
However, the marginal-contribution profile does \emph{not} match the
distributed prediction.
Instead, NER is \textbf{concentrated at L3} (JSON format specification):
L2$\to$L3 accounts for $+0.750$ quality, or 96\% of the total
L1$\to$L7 gain ($+0.788$).
Four of five models score exactly $q = 0.000$ at L1--L2---complete task
failure---then jump to $q > 0.90$ at L3 when the output format is
specified.
Subsequent layers (definitions, persona, guidelines, worked example)
add nothing.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Our prediction was \emph{partially} correct: NER is prompt-sensitive
(massive L1$\to$L7 gain, 4/5 significant fits), not insensitive like
QA or math.
But the specific category---distributed---was wrong.
NER behaves as a \textbf{concentrated} task, where a single format
instruction unlocks the task entirely.
The key difference from product extraction is \emph{schema complexity}:
NER requires three homogeneous entity lists, while product extraction
requires four heterogeneous fields (name, price, brand, category) that
benefit from definitions and worked examples.
This refines the gradient taxonomy: the boundary between concentrated
and distributed depends on schema complexity, not merely on whether the
task involves structured output.
The held-out experiment thus improves the framework rather than merely
confirming it.

\paragraph{Per-dimension decomposition.}
Decomposing the aggregate quality $q = \sum s_i / 20$ into its four
subscores reveals which dimensions drive saturation.
For product extraction, \textbf{completeness} accounts for 43\% of the
total L1$\to$L7 gain (+1.40 on the 1--5 subscale), confirming that

+0.69, completeness +0.61, reasoning +0.56, conciseness +0.71)---
concentrated in \emph{tokens} but distributed across \emph{dimensions}.

($-0.72$) as extra prompt layers add noise to already-correct answers.

\subsection{Threshold Sensitivity}
\label{sec:result-threshold}

For classification, saturation points are stable across threshold choices:
the median 90\%-to-95\% shift across all 7 models is 3 tokens, and 6 of 7
models shift by fewer than 11 tokens.
One outlier---qwen3-32b---follows a logarithmic rather than sigmoid
trajectory, producing a 95-token shift.
This model uniquely benefits from the guidelines layer (L6), suggesting it
integrates detailed instructions more gradually.
Its threshold-free knee estimate (42 tokens) falls close to the other
models', confirming similar saturation onset despite the different curve
shape.

\subsection{Replication on Larger Dataset}
\label{sec:replication}

The 200-example replication confirms the main-study patterns.
Classification again exhibits a clear sigmoid relationship, with the
estimated saturation point at approximately 64 tokens---consistent with
the main experiment.
QA shows no significant relationship between prompt length and quality,
reinforcing the insensitive classification.

\subsection{Sampling Stability}
\label{sec:result-stability}

To assess whether findings are robust to sampling variance, we repeat the
full experiment (3 gradient-critical tasks $\times$ 7 levels $\times$ 20
examples) twice more with the same temperature-zero setting on
llama-3.1-8b and gemini-flash.
Across three runs of llama-3.1-8b (same model version throughout), the
L1$\to$L7 quality delta is highly stable:
classification $\Delta = +0.190, +0.235, +0.245$
(std $= 0.024$),
product extraction $\Delta = +0.097, +0.133, +0.120$
(std $= 0.015$),
instruction following $\Delta = +0.062, -0.005, +0.000$
(std $= 0.031$).
Classification and product extraction show positive gains in all three
runs; instruction following hovers near zero, consistent with its
``diffuse'' classification.
L7 quality is especially stable (e.g., classification: 0.970 across all
three runs), with most variance concentrated at L1 where the bare input
gives the model less guidance.

Gemini-flash repeated runs used gemini-2.5-flash (the original
gemini-2.0-flash was deprecated between study phases).
Runs 1 and 2 (both gemini-2.5-flash) are near-identical
(classification $\Delta = +0.345$ vs.\ $+0.347$), confirming
temperature-zero determinism, while the gap from run~0 (gemini-2.0-flash,
$\Delta = +0.110$) reflects the model version change rather than sampling
noise.
Crucially, the \emph{direction} of all effects is preserved across both
model versions: all three tasks show positive L1$\to$L7 gains under both
gemini-2.0-flash and gemini-2.5-flash.
