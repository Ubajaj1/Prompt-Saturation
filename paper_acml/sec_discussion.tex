\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Information Sufficiency, Not Token Budgets}

The convergence of three independent experiments---the core saturation study,
the randomised ablation, and the padding control---supports a single
mechanism: quality saturates once \emph{task-critical information} has been
communicated.
The saturation \emph{shape} (quality rising then plateauing) is robust across
prompt orderings, but the specific token count at which it occurs is not---it
shifts with which layers appear early.
Irrelevant tokens at matched counts produce flat or declining quality.
The practical implication is that prompt optimisation should focus on
\emph{which mechanisms to include}, not on \emph{how many tokens to budget}.

\subsection{Schema Compliance as a Mechanism}

Three converging lines of evidence support the hypothesis that structured tasks
saturate because quality is governed by schema compliance---the model's
ability to produce a response in the required format:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Marginal contributions:} The schema-defining layers dominate
    quality gain. For classification, L1$\to$L2 (task label) and L2$\to$L3
    (format spec) account for 61\% of total gain. For product extraction,
    L6$\to$L7 (worked example demonstrating output schema) accounts for 40\%.
  \item \textbf{Ceiling stratification:} Open-ended tasks (QA, math) reach
    ceiling at L1 precisely because they do \emph{not} require schema
    compliance---the model already knows what to output.
  \item \textbf{Output compression:} Classification output drops from 85
    tokens (L1, verbose explanation) to 2 tokens (L3+, just the label) as
    format specification takes effect. Quality tracks this compression.
\end{enumerate}

We present schema compliance as a hypothesis supported by converging evidence
rather than a directly tested mechanism.
Mechanistic interpretability analyses of how models process elaboration levels
could provide a direct test.

\subsection{Harmful Prompt Content}

The math L3 finding---instructing models to ``give only the final numerical
answer'' destroys quality by $-0.366$ across all seven models and both
judges---demonstrates that prompt content can be actively harmful.
This extends beyond the ``diminishing returns'' framing: certain instructions
are not merely unhelpful but counterproductive.
The standard sigmoid saturation model does not capture tasks where
intermediate prompt layers are detrimental, and this non-monotonicity is
important for practitioners to be aware of.

\subsection{Practical Guidelines}

The prompt-sensitivity gradient provides concrete, task-specific guidance:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Classification (concentrated):} Specify the task label and
    output format ($\approx$50 tokens). Adding worked examples
    ($\approx$300+ tokens) provides no benefit for most models.
  \item \textbf{Structured extraction (distributed):} Include a worked
    example demonstrating the expected output format. Models vary
    substantially (92--536 tokens); weaker models require more schema
    detail while stronger models infer the schema from minimal cues.
  \item \textbf{Instruction following (diffuse):} Small gains spread across
    many layers; no single mechanism dominates.
  \item \textbf{QA and math reasoning (insensitive):} The bare question
    suffices. Invest in retrieval-augmented generation (for QA) or
    chain-of-thought formatting (for reasoning) rather than instruction
    elaboration. \emph{Avoid} suppressing step-by-step reasoning for math.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Relationship to Prompt Compression}

Our work and prompt compression methods (LLMLingua, LLMLingua-2) operate at
different stages of the prompt lifecycle.
Compression decides what to keep \emph{given a written prompt}; our marginal
contribution analysis decides what to \emph{write in the first place}.
For classification, LLMLingua-2 might compress L7 ($\approx$180 tokens) to
$\approx$36 tokens by removing filler---but our analysis shows L1$\to$L2
(adding $\approx$5 tokens of task label) already captures 49\% of the quality
gain, suggesting the L7 elaboration was largely unnecessary content rather
than redundantly worded content.
The two approaches are complementary and stack: author the right mechanisms
(our work), then compress the wording within them (prompt compression).

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Statistical power.}
With $n = 7$ data points per curve, the F-test has limited power.
Borderline cases (classification for llama-3.1-8b at $p = 0.079$; product
extraction for kimi-k2 at $p = 0.093$) are plausibly true positives that
the marginal contribution analysis confirms as prompt-sensitive.
Future work should use finer-grained level spacing (15--20 levels).

\paragraph{Classification domain breadth.}
Classification results draw from sentiment analysis only.
Product extraction provides a second structurally distinct task with the
same qualitative saturation pattern, but extending to additional
classification domains (topic, intent, category) would strengthen
generalisability.

\paragraph{Single-turn scope.}
The study focuses on single-turn instruction tasks.
Agentic, RAG, and multi-turn settings may exhibit different saturation
dynamics---our mechanism-level findings yield testable predictions for
these settings (e.g., tool-call format specifications may exhibit
concentrated sensitivity analogous to classification).

\paragraph{English only.}
All prompts and examples are in English.
Saturation behaviour may differ for low-resource languages where prompt
elaboration is more necessary to disambiguate tasks.

\paragraph{Model deprecation.}
kimi-k2 was deprecated by Groq between the main study (March 2026) and
the ablation experiments (May 2026), producing 404 errors on all 490
ablation attempts.
Main-study data from kimi-k2 is retained (recorded outputs are immutable),
and the ablation analyses are restricted to the 5 models with complete data.

\paragraph{Post-hoc gradient.}
The prompt-sensitivity gradient (concentrated $\to$ distributed $\to$
diffuse $\to$ insensitive) is a post-hoc descriptive framework supported by
three independent analyses (ceiling stratification, marginal contributions,
ablation), not a pre-registered taxonomy.
Validation as a predictive framework requires testing on new tasks.
